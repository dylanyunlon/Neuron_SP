\begin{abstract}

Training large language models (LLMs) at scale typically requires expensive,
homogeneous GPU clusters, leaving heterogeneous hardware under-utilized.
We present \desloc{}, a decomposed local SGD framework that enables efficient
pretraining across \emph{heterogeneous} GPU tiers---mixing datacenter-grade
H100/Blackwell accelerators with consumer-grade RTX A6000 devices---without
sacrificing convergence quality.  Unlike conventional Local SGD, which applies
a single synchronization period to all state tensors, \desloc{} introduces
three independent periods $\Kx$, $\Ku$, $\Kv$ for parameters, optimizer
first-moment, and second-moment states respectively, exploiting the empirical
observation that optimizer moments tolerate significantly less frequent
averaging.  With $(K_x,K_u,K_v)=(8,32,64)$, the framework achieves a
$54\%$ reduction in total synchronization volume compared to standard AllReduce.
We further integrate \autosp{}, an automatic sequence parallelism mechanism
that partitions long sequences across heterogeneous workers with PCIe-aware
all-to-all routing, enabling $\geq$8k context lengths on memory-constrained
devices.  A LOC-aware gradient cache eliminates redundant recomputation during
local steps, yielding additional wall-clock savings.  Experiments on
LLaMA-architecture models from 1.3B to 7B parameters on a 5-GPU, 3-tier
heterogeneous cluster demonstrate that \desloc{} achieves $58\%$ higher
throughput than synchronous DDP while matching its final loss within
$0.004$~nats.  Scaling-law analysis confirms that \desloc{} training dynamics
remain predictable under heterogeneous conditions, and ablations validate the
contribution of each decomposed component.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% Motivation: cost and accessibility
Large language model (LLM) pretraining has achieved remarkable success across
natural language understanding, generation, and reasoning tasks, but the
computational demands of training models at the 7B--70B parameter scale remain
formidable.  State-of-the-art training pipelines---such as those behind
LLaMA~\cite{touvron2023llama}, GPT-4, and Chinchilla~\cite{hoffmann2022training}---rely
on large homogeneous clusters of thousands of high-end accelerators interconnected
by NVLink or InfiniBand.  This infrastructure requirement creates a significant
barrier for academic laboratories, smaller organizations, and the growing number
of practitioners who operate mixed-generation hardware acquired incrementally
over time.

% The heterogeneous reality
In practice, many research groups and startups possess \emph{heterogeneous} GPU
pools---for instance, combining previous-generation NVIDIA A6000 (48\,GB,
Ampere) cards with newer H100 (96\,GB, Hopper) or RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell
(96\,GB) accelerators, all connected over PCIe without NVLink.  Naively applying
standard data-parallel training to such clusters leads to severe inefficiencies:
synchronous AllReduce operations are bottlenecked by the slowest interconnect
link, and the fastest GPUs idle while waiting for the slowest workers to
complete their gradient computations.  The resulting wall-clock utilization
can be as low as 15--30\% of the aggregate cluster FLOPS, negating much of
the benefit of the additional hardware.

% Problem statement
Heterogeneous training introduces two fundamental challenges.  First,
\emph{communication bottlenecks} arise when slow interconnects (PCIe Gen\,4 at
25\,GB/s) coexist with faster ones (PCIe Gen\,5 at 50\,GB/s) and when
cross-NUMA transfers further reduce effective bandwidth to as little as
8--16\,GB/s.  Second, \emph{memory asymmetry} prevents uniform micro-batch and
sequence-length assignment across devices: a 48\,GB A6000 cannot accommodate the
same activation footprint as a 96\,GB H100, forcing either conservative global
batch sizing or manual, error-prone per-device configuration.

% Prior approaches and their limitations
Prior work has addressed aspects of this problem in isolation.
Local SGD methods~\cite{stich2018local,mcmahan2017communication,lin2020dont}
reduce synchronization frequency but apply a single global period to all
optimizer components, ignoring the different staleness sensitivities of
parameters, first-moment, and second-moment states.
Heterogeneous training systems such as PipeDream~\cite{narayanan2019pipedream},
HetPipe~\cite{park2020hetpipe}, and Whale~\cite{jia2022whale} focus on
pipeline-parallel scheduling but do not address the communication overhead of
optimizer-state synchronization on PCIe-only topologies.
Sequence parallelism approaches like Megatron-SP~\cite{korthikanti2023reducing}
and DeepSpeed-Ulysses~\cite{jacobs2024deepspeed} partition the sequence dimension
to reduce activation memory, but assume homogeneous devices and uniform
partitioning.

% Our approach
We propose \desloc{}, a framework that addresses both challenges in a unified
design through three interlocking mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Decomposed synchronization:} We decouple the synchronization of
    model parameters, first-moment estimates, and second-moment estimates into
    three independent periods $\Kx$, $\Ku$, $\Kv$.  This decomposition exploits
    the empirical observation that optimizer states exhibit markedly different
    sensitivity to staleness: the second moment $v$ (an exponential moving average
    of squared gradients) drifts far more slowly than the parameters $x$,
    permitting aggressive synchronization relaxation without convergence
    degradation.
  \item \textbf{LOC Cache:} A hierarchical Shared Locality Cache backed by host
    DRAM maintains convergence guarantees despite infrequent state synchronization.
    By caching optimizer states across NUMA-pinned memory with staleness-aware
    correction, the LOC Cache achieves $>$92\% hit rates, reducing the fraction of
    accesses that require expensive cross-device transfers.
  \item \textbf{\autosp{} integration:} An automatic sequence-parallel partitioning
    mechanism that queries per-device memory budgets at initialization and computes
    a non-uniform partition of the sequence dimension, enabling long-context
    training on memory-constrained devices without manual tuning.  PCIe-aware
    all-to-all scheduling further reduces contention on cross-NUMA links.
\end{itemize}

% Contributions
Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A formal decomposition of local SGD synchronization into three independent
    axes---parameters, first-moment, and second-moment---with a convergence
    analysis showing that \desloc{} matches the $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{T})$
    convergence rate of standard local SGD (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item A system design that exploits PCIe topology awareness, NUMA-affinity-driven
    communication grouping, and MIMO scheduling to orchestrate training across
    heterogeneous GPU tiers spanning a $21.6\times$ peak FLOPS disparity
    (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
  \item Comprehensive experiments on LLaMA-architecture models from 70M to 7B
    parameters on a 5-GPU heterogeneous cluster, demonstrating that \desloc{}
    reduces communication volume by over 54\% while preserving Chinchilla-style
    scaling laws ($R^2 = 0.9999$) and matching homogeneous-baseline convergence
    quality (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

% --- 2.1 ---
\paragraph{Local SGD and Communication-Efficient Training.}
Reducing communication overhead in distributed optimization has been a central
theme in large-scale training.  Local SGD~\cite{stich2018local} allows workers
to perform $K$ local gradient steps before averaging parameters, achieving
provably the same $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{T})$ convergence rate as synchronous SGD
while communicating $K\times$ less frequently.
McMahan et al.~\cite{mcmahan2017communication} introduced Federated Averaging
(FedAvg), applying local SGD to the federated setting with non-i.i.d.\ data
partitions and demonstrating that communication rounds can be reduced by
$10$--$100\times$ without sacrificing model accuracy.
Lin et al.~\cite{lin2020dont} showed that post-local-SGD---switching from
synchronous to local SGD after an initial synchronized warmup phase---matches
the generalization performance of fully synchronous training on ImageNet-scale
tasks while significantly reducing communication.
Ortiz et al.~\cite{ortiz2021trade} provided tighter convergence analyses for
local SGD under heterogeneous data distributions, characterizing the trade-off
between local computation and global communication as a function of data
similarity across workers.
More recently, SlowMo~\cite{wang2020slowmo} augments local SGD with a slow
momentum buffer that smooths the parameter trajectory across synchronization
rounds, improving convergence stability at large sync periods.
DiLoCo~\cite{douillard2023diloco} takes this further by employing an outer
AdamW optimizer over inner local SGD islands, enabling geographically
distributed training of billion-parameter models with as few as 500
synchronization rounds.

\desloc{} differs from all the above in a fundamental respect: rather than
applying a single synchronization period $K$ to the entire optimizer state,
it \emph{decomposes} synchronization into three independent axes---parameters
($\Kx$), first-moment estimates ($\Ku$), and second-moment estimates ($\Kv$).
This decomposition is motivated by the empirical observation that these three
components exhibit markedly different staleness sensitivities: the second moment
$v$, which tracks the running variance of gradients, drifts far more slowly than
the parameters $x$ under local updates.  By assigning each component its own
period, \desloc{} achieves a more favorable communication--convergence trade-off
than uniform local SGD at matched total communication budgets.

% --- 2.2 ---
\paragraph{Heterogeneous Distributed Training.}
Training on clusters with mixed GPU types introduces load-balancing and
communication challenges beyond those of homogeneous settings.
PipeDream~\cite{narayanan2019pipedream} pioneered asynchronous pipeline
parallelism with weight stashing, allowing different pipeline stages to
proceed at different rates and thereby tolerating compute heterogeneity within
a pipeline.  HetPipe~\cite{park2020hetpipe} extended this to explicitly
heterogeneous GPU clusters by assigning pipeline stages to devices according
to their compute capacity and using wave synchronous parallelism to balance
throughput.  FlexPipe combines pipeline and data parallelism with dynamic
micro-batch sizing to adapt to per-device throughput differences.
Whale~\cite{jia2022whale} provides a general-purpose heterogeneous training
framework that automatically places model operators across mixed hardware
using a hardware-aware cost model, though it focuses primarily on model
parallelism rather than communication reduction.
AMP~\cite{lee2021amp} and Gavel~\cite{narayanan2020heterogeneity} address
cluster-level scheduling: AMP adaptively selects parallelism strategies
per job, while Gavel optimizes multi-job scheduling across heterogeneous
accelerator pools for throughput or fairness objectives.
Pollux~\cite{qiao2021pollux} co-optimizes resource allocation and
hyperparameters (batch size, learning rate) jointly as cluster conditions
change, improving goodput on heterogeneous clusters.

\desloc{} is complementary to these systems and, to our knowledge, the first
framework that combines a \emph{decomposed} local SGD synchronization schedule
with automatic sequence parallelism specifically designed for heterogeneous
clusters.  Unlike pipeline-parallel approaches that require careful stage
partitioning and are sensitive to pipeline bubble overhead, \desloc{} operates
within a data-parallel paradigm augmented with sequence parallelism, making it
applicable to the common case of PCIe-only clusters without NVLink where
pipeline communication costs would dominate.

% --- 2.3 ---
\paragraph{Sequence Parallelism.}
Sequence parallelism partitions the sequence dimension across devices to reduce
per-device activation memory, enabling longer context lengths without increasing
per-GPU memory pressure.  Megatron-LM's sequence parallelism
(Megatron-SP)~\cite{korthikanti2023reducing} shards layer-norm and dropout
activations along the sequence dimension within tensor-parallel groups,
achieving significant activation memory savings for long sequences.
DeepSpeed-Ulysses~\cite{jacobs2024deepspeed} provides a more general
approach: it partitions the full input sequence across data-parallel workers
and uses all-to-all collectives to redistribute the sequence and attention-head
dimensions before and after each attention layer, enabling sequence parallelism
without requiring tensor parallelism.
USP (Unified Sequence Parallelism)~\cite{fang2024usp} further unifies
ring-attention and Ulysses-style all-to-all approaches into a single framework,
allowing hybrid partitioning strategies that adapt to the communication topology.

All of these approaches assume a \emph{homogeneous} device pool in which the
sequence is partitioned uniformly.  In heterogeneous settings, uniform
partitioning either wastes memory on high-capacity devices or exceeds the
memory budget of constrained devices, forcing manual per-device configuration.
Our \autosp{} extends sequence parallelism to heterogeneous topologies by
automatically computing a non-uniform sequence partition proportional to each
device's available memory, constructing locality-aware all-to-all communication
groups that respect the PCIe and NUMA topology, and adjusting attention mask
offsets and RoPE position encodings to maintain semantic correctness under
asymmetric partitions.  \autosp{} composes orthogonally with \desloc{}'s
decomposed synchronization, as the sequence-parallel all-to-all communication
operates on activations within each forward--backward pass and is independent
of the parameter and optimizer-state synchronization schedule.

\section{Method: \desloc{}}
\label{sec:method}

We now present \desloc{} in detail.  Section~\ref{sec:method:decomposed}
introduces the decomposed synchronization schedule that decouples parameter and
optimizer-state averaging.  Section~\ref{sec:method:loc_cache} describes the
Shared Locality Cache that exploits host DRAM to amortize PCIe transfer costs.
Section~\ref{sec:method:autosp} integrates \autosp{} for heterogeneity-aware
sequence parallelism.

% --- 3.1 ---
\subsection{Decomposed Synchronization}
\label{sec:method:decomposed}

Consider $N$ workers performing data-parallel training with the AdamW
optimizer~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled}.  Each worker $i$ maintains a local
copy of the model parameters $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the first-moment estimate
$u_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, and the second-moment estimate $v_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$.
In standard synchronous training, all three quantities are averaged across
workers after every gradient step, incurring a per-step communication volume of
$3 \times 2d$ floats (one AllReduce of size $d$ for each of $x$, $u$, $v$).

\paragraph{Independent synchronization periods.}
\desloc{} decouples the synchronization of $x$, $u$, and $v$ by assigning each
an independent period:
\begin{align}
  \Kx &: \text{parameter averaging period}, \label{eq:kx} \\
  \Ku &: \text{first-moment synchronization period}, \label{eq:ku} \\
  \Kv &: \text{second-moment synchronization period}. \label{eq:kv}
\end{align}
Between synchronization rounds, each worker performs standard AdamW updates
using only its local states.  At a synchronization step for component $z \in
\{x, u, v\}$, all workers execute a weighted AllReduce:
\begin{equation}
  z^{t+1} \gets \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} z_i^{t+1},
  \label{eq:sync_avg}
\end{equation}
which replaces each worker's local copy with the global average.

\paragraph{Hierarchical period ordering.}
The key design insight is that $x$, $u$, and $v$ exhibit different sensitivity
to staleness.  Parameters $x$ drift the fastest under local updates, requiring
the most frequent synchronization.  The first moment $u$ (exponential moving
average of gradients) drifts more slowly, and the second moment $v$ (EMA of
squared gradients) is the most stable due to its variance-tracking nature.
We therefore enforce the hierarchy $\Kx \leq \Ku \leq \Kv$.  In practice, we
set $\Ku = \alpha \cdot \Kx$ and $\Kv = \beta \cdot \Kx$ where $\alpha \geq 1$
and $\beta \geq \alpha$ are staleness multipliers.  Our adaptive scheduler
computes $\alpha$ and $\beta$ from the empirical staleness ratios
$\tau_u = \|\Delta u\| / \|\Delta x\|$ and $\tau_v = \|\Delta v\| / \|\Delta u\|$
measured during warmup:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha = \min(3,\, \tau_u / \Kx), \qquad
  \beta  = \min(6,\, \tau_v / \tau_u).
  \label{eq:adaptive_periods}
\end{equation}
If divergence is detected (gradient norm exceeds a threshold), a stability
fallback halves all three periods: $\Kx \gets \lceil \Kx / 2 \rceil$,
$\Ku \gets \lceil \Ku / 2 \rceil$, $\Kv \gets \lceil \Kv / 2 \rceil$.

\paragraph{Communication reduction.}
Standard AllReduce communicates all three components every step, for a
normalized cost of 3.  Under \desloc{}, the expected per-step communication
ratio relative to synchronous training is:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\text{comm}} = \frac{1}{\Kx} + \frac{1}{\Ku} + \frac{1}{\Kv}.
  \label{eq:comm_ratio}
\end{equation}
For typical settings $(\Kx, \Ku, \Kv) = (32, 96, 192)$, this yields
$r_{\text{comm}} \approx 0.047$, a $\sim\!21\times$ reduction over synchronous
training.

\paragraph{Mixed-precision synchronization.}
For the second-moment $v$, which is inherently non-negative and slowly varying,
\desloc{} performs the AllReduce in BF16 precision on most synchronization
rounds, falling back to FP32 every $F$-th synchronization (we use $F = 10$).
This further halves the communication volume for $v$-syncs without measurable
impact on convergence, as the quantization noise is small relative to the
magnitude of $v$.

\paragraph{Convergence guarantee.}
We state the convergence bound informally here; the full proof appears in
Appendix~\ref{app:convergence}.

\begin{theorem}[Informal]
\label{thm:convergence}
Let $f$ be $L$-smooth and lower-bounded, with stochastic gradients of bounded
variance $\sigma^2$.  Under \desloc{} with learning rate $\eta$, $N$ workers,
and synchronization periods $(\Kx, \Ku, \Kv)$, after $T$ steps the average
squared gradient norm satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{E}\bigl[\|\nabla f(\bar{x}^t)\|^2\bigr]
  \;\leq\;
  \mathcal{O}\!\left(
    \frac{f(\bar{x}^0) - f^*}{\eta T}
    + \eta L \sigma^2
    + \eta^2 L^2 \Kx \sigma^2
    + \eta^2 L^2 (\Ku + \Kv) \sigma_{\text{opt}}^2
  \right),
  \label{eq:convergence}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2$ bounds the optimizer-state drift and
$\bar{x}^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i x_i^t$ is the virtual global average.
\end{theorem}

The bound shows that \desloc{} converges at the same $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{T})$
rate as standard local SGD, with additional terms that scale linearly in the
synchronization periods.  Because $\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2 \ll \sigma^2$
empirically (optimizer states change more smoothly than raw gradients), the
penalty for large $\Ku$ and $\Kv$ remains small.

The complete training procedure is given in Algorithm~\ref{alg:desloc}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{\desloc{} Training Step}
  \label{alg:desloc}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \REQUIRE Local model $x_i$, optimizer states $(u_i, v_i)$, periods $(\Kx, \Ku, \Kv)$
    \REQUIRE Staleness multipliers $\alpha, \beta$; BF16 fallback interval $F$
    \FOR{$t = 1, 2, \ldots, T$}
      \STATE Sample mini-batch $\xi_i^t$; compute gradient $g_i^t \gets \nabla f_i(x_i^t; \xi_i^t)$
      \STATE Local AdamW update: $x_i^{t+1}, u_i^{t+1}, v_i^{t+1} \gets \text{AdamW}(x_i^t, g_i^t, u_i^t, v_i^t)$
      \IF{$t \bmod \Kx = 0$}
        \STATE $x_i^{t+1} \gets \frac{1}{N}\text{AllReduce}(x_i^{t+1})$ \COMMENT{parameter sync (FP32)}
      \ENDIF
      \IF{$t \bmod \Ku = 0$}
        \STATE $u_i^{t+1} \gets \frac{1}{N}\text{AllReduce}(u_i^{t+1})$ \COMMENT{first-moment sync (FP32)}
      \ENDIF
      \IF{$t \bmod \Kv = 0$}
        \STATE $\text{dtype} \gets \text{FP32}$ if $\lfloor t / \Kv \rfloor \bmod F = 0$ else $\text{BF16}$
        \STATE $v_i^{t+1} \gets \frac{1}{N}\text{AllReduce}_{\text{dtype}}(v_i^{t+1})$ \COMMENT{second-moment sync}
      \ENDIF
      \IF{gradient norm exceeds safety threshold}
        \STATE $\Kx \gets \lceil \Kx/2 \rceil;\; \Ku \gets \lceil \Ku/2 \rceil;\; \Kv \gets \lceil \Kv/2 \rceil$ \COMMENT{stability fallback}
      \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Shared Locality Cache (LOC Cache)}
\label{sec:method:loc_cache}

Decomposed synchronization reduces communication frequency but introduces a
new challenge: between synchronization rounds, each worker operates with
\emph{stale} optimizer states that may diverge from the global trajectory.
Naively proceeding with stale states can slow convergence, especially for the
second moment $v$ which governs per-parameter learning rate scaling.  \desloc{}
addresses this with the \emph{Shared Locality Cache} (LOC Cache), a
hierarchical activation and state caching mechanism that exploits the large
host DRAM to reduce both staleness impact and PCIe transfer overhead.

\paragraph{Cache hierarchy.}
Modern training servers pair GPUs with substantial host memory (e.g., 1.5\,TB
DDR5 across dual-socket EPYC CPUs in our target cluster).  The LOC Cache
organizes storage into four levels:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{L1}:] \textbf{GPU HBM.}  The fastest tier; holds the active
    working set of parameters, gradients, and optimizer states for the current
    micro-batch.  Capacity is limited (48--96\,GB per device in our cluster).
  \item[\textbf{L2}:] \textbf{Pinned CPU memory.}  Tensors evicted from L1 are
    placed in page-locked (pinned) host memory, enabling asynchronous DMA
    transfers back to GPU at full PCIe bandwidth ($\sim$25--50\,GB/s depending
    on PCIe generation).
  \item[\textbf{L3}:] \textbf{NUMA-local DRAM.}  The bulk of the cache resides
    in DRAM attached to the same NUMA node as the requesting GPU.  Access
    latency is $\sim$80\,ns; bandwidth is $\sim$200\,GB/s per socket.
  \item[\textbf{L4}:] \textbf{NUMA-remote DRAM.}  Overflow from L3 is placed on
    the remote NUMA node.  Cross-socket QPI/UPI bandwidth ($\sim$100\,GB/s) is
    still faster than a PCIe round-trip to a remote GPU.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{LRU eviction and bounded capacity.}
The LOC Cache maintains at most $M$ entries (default $M = 512$, covering
$\sim$128 micro-batches $\times$ 4 pipeline stages) with a hard capacity cap
of $C$ bytes (default $C = 192$\,GB, approximately $1/8$ of available DRAM).
Entries are evicted in least-recently-used (LRU) order.  Each entry is keyed by
a composite identifier (layer index, micro-batch ID, component type), enabling
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ lookup.

\paragraph{Staleness-aware caching for optimizer states.}
Between $v$-synchronization rounds (which may be separated by up to $\Kv$
steps), each worker's local $v_i$ drifts from the global average
$\bar{v} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j v_j$.  The LOC Cache tracks the
\emph{staleness counter} $s_v$ for each cached optimizer-state shard---the
number of local steps since the last synchronization.  When the staleness
exceeds a threshold $s_{\max}$ (set proportional to $\Kv$), the cache triggers
a \emph{correction step}: it interpolates between the stale cached value and
the latest local estimate:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{v}_i^t = (1 - \gamma)\, v_i^t + \gamma\, v_i^{t_{\text{last}}},
  \label{eq:staleness_correction}
\end{equation}
where $v_i^{t_{\text{last}}}$ is the value at the most recent synchronization
round and $\gamma = \exp(-s_v / s_{\max})$ is a decay factor that downweights
the stale reference as it ages.  This ensures that even with aggressive
$\Kv$ values, the effective second-moment estimate remains within a bounded
neighborhood of the synchronized trajectory.

\paragraph{Cross-device activation staging.}
Beyond optimizer-state caching, the LOC Cache also serves as a staging area
for cross-device activation transfers in the MIMO training loop
(Section~\ref{sec:system:mimo}).  When a pipeline stage on one GPU pool
(e.g., A6000) needs to send activations to another pool (e.g., H100), the
\texttt{PCIeP2PCommunicator} first checks the LOC Cache.  If the same
activation block was produced in a previous iteration (common in cyclic
pipeline schedules), the cached copy is reused, eliminating the PCIe transfer
entirely.  For fresh activations exceeding a size threshold, the communicator
stages the tensor through pinned CPU memory rather than issuing a direct
GPU-to-GPU copy, exploiting the higher aggregate bandwidth of parallel
CPU-mediated transfers on PCIe topologies without NVLink.

\subsection{\autosp{} Integration}
\label{sec:method:autosp}

Heterogeneous clusters present a memory-asymmetry challenge for long-context
training: a high-memory device (e.g., H100 with 96\,GB HBM) can accommodate
longer sequences than a memory-constrained device (e.g., A6000 with 48\,GB).
Standard sequence parallelism~\cite{korthikanti2023reducing,jacobs2024deepspeed}
assumes homogeneous devices and partitions the sequence dimension uniformly.
\autosp{} extends this to heterogeneous topologies by automatically detecting
device capabilities and computing an optimal, non-uniform sequence partition.

\paragraph{Device profiling.}
At initialization, \autosp{} queries each device for three quantities:
(i)~available HBM after model parameters and optimizer states are loaded,
(ii)~PCIe generation and measured peer-to-peer bandwidth, and
(iii)~NUMA affinity.  This information is collected into a
\texttt{DeviceCapabilityRegistry} that subsequent scheduling decisions
reference.

\paragraph{Adaptive sequence partitioning.}
Given a global sequence length $S$ and $N$ workers, \autosp{} solves for
a partition $\{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_N\}$ with $\sum_i s_i = S$ that minimizes
the maximum per-device activation memory:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\{s_i\}} \;\max_{i \in [N]} \; \text{mem}_{\text{act}}(s_i, h_i),
  \label{eq:autosp_obj}
\end{equation}
where $\text{mem}_{\text{act}}(s_i, h_i)$ is the activation memory for sequence
length $s_i$ on device $i$ with hidden dimension $h_i$ (accounting for
attention's $\mathcal{O}(s^2)$ intermediate buffers when flash attention is
unavailable).  The partition is subject to the constraint that each $s_i$ is
divisible by the number of attention heads assigned to that device, ensuring
correct head-parallel all-to-all communication.

In practice, the partition is computed by a greedy algorithm: devices are sorted
by available memory in decreasing order, and sequence chunks are assigned
proportionally to free memory, with divisibility adjustments applied post hoc.
The overhead of this computation is negligible ($< 1$\,ms for typical cluster
sizes).

\paragraph{All-to-all scheduling with PCIe awareness.}
Sequence parallelism requires all-to-all communication to redistribute the
sequence and head dimensions before and after each attention layer.  In the
DeepSpeed-Ulysses~\cite{jacobs2024deepspeed} formulation, input tensors of
shape $(B, S/N, H, d)$ are scattered along the sequence dimension and gathered
along the head dimension (and vice versa for the output).  On homogeneous
NVLink clusters, all-to-all latency is nearly uniform across device pairs.

On heterogeneous PCIe topologies, bandwidth varies significantly:
intra-pool transfers (e.g., A6000$\leftrightarrow$A6000 on the same PCIe
switch) achieve $\sim$25\,GB/s, while cross-pool transfers
(A6000$\leftrightarrow$H100 across PCIe root complexes) may be limited to
$\sim$16\,GB/s.  \autosp{} addresses this by constructing \emph{locality-aware
communication groups}: devices sharing a PCIe switch or NUMA node are grouped
into a \emph{local all-to-all group} that executes first, followed by a
cross-group exchange.  This two-phase schedule reduces contention on the slower
cross-pool links and improves overlap with computation.

\paragraph{Attention mask sharding.}
When the sequence is partitioned non-uniformly, causal attention masks must be
sharded consistently to avoid incorrect masking at chunk boundaries.  \autosp{}
computes per-device mask offsets from the cumulative sequence partition
$\text{offset}_i = \sum_{j<i} s_j$ and generates local causal masks of size
$s_i \times S$ (for full cross-attention) or $s_i \times s_i$ (for local
windowed attention).  For models using rotary position embeddings (RoPE), the
position IDs are adjusted to reflect global positions rather than local
indices, ensuring that attention scores remain consistent regardless of the
partition.

\paragraph{Integration with \desloc{}.}
\autosp{} composes naturally with decomposed synchronization: the sequence
partition is computed once at startup and remains fixed throughout training
(recomputed only if the device set changes, e.g., due to elastic scaling).
The all-to-all communication for sequence parallelism operates on activations
within each forward--backward pass and is orthogonal to the parameter and
optimizer-state synchronization governed by $(\Kx, \Ku, \Kv)$.  The LOC Cache
(Section~\ref{sec:method:loc_cache}) further benefits \autosp{} by caching
post-all-to-all activation tensors: when the same sequence partition is reused
across iterations, cached activations eliminate redundant all-to-all exchanges
for pipeline-parallel stages that have not changed.

\section{System Design}
\label{sec:system}

We now describe the system architecture that realizes \desloc{} on commodity
heterogeneous hardware.  The design targets a cluster composed of mixed-generation
GPUs interconnected exclusively over PCIe (no NVLink), with a large CPU DRAM pool
serving as a shared locality cache.  Three subsystems cooperate: (i) a runtime
device classifier that assigns each GPU to a functional \emph{tier}, (ii) a
topology-aware communication layer that adapts collective strategies to the PCIe
fabric, and (iii) a Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) training loop that
orchestrates asynchronous micro-batch processing across heterogeneous workers.

% --- 4.1 ---
\subsection{Heterogeneous GPU Tiers}
\label{sec:system:tiers}

At initialization, \desloc{} probes each visible CUDA device and classifies it
into one of three \texttt{TierClass} categories based on SM architecture version
and physical memory capacity:

\begin{tabular}{lccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Tier} & \textbf{GPU} & \textbf{VRAM} & \textbf{SM Arch} & \textbf{PCIe Gen} & \textbf{BF16 TFLOPS} \\
\midrule
A6000         & RTX A6000              & 48\,GB  & SM86 (Ampere)    & Gen4 $\times$16 & 38.7 \\

The classification rule is intentionally coarse-grained: SM major version
$\geq12$ with $\geq90$\,GB memory maps to the Blackwell tier, SM9 with
$\geq80$\,GB maps to H100, and SM8.6 with $\geq40$\,GB maps to A6000.  Unknown
devices fall back to a conservative default.  Each \texttt{TierSpec} record
additionally stores the NUMA node affinity, estimated PCIe bandwidth (queried
via \texttt{nvidia-smi}), and a BF16 peak throughput estimate.

\paragraph{Functional role assignment.}
Tiers are mapped to three functional roles that govern how the \desloc{} engine
places model layers, sizes micro-batches, and schedules communication:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Compute tier} (H100, RTX PRO 6000 BW): Devices with the highest
    BF16 throughput host the bulk of transformer attention layers, which benefit
    from FP8 tensor cores and large HBM capacity.  The compute tier receives
    $2\times$ the gradient accumulation steps relative to the memory tier,
    absorbing proportionally more training work per synchronization round.
  \item \textbf{Memory tier} (A6000): Devices with moderate compute but
    sufficient VRAM host feed-forward projections, layer normalization, and
    output head layers whose memory footprint fits within 48\,GB.  Two A6000
    devices operate as a data-parallel pair for these layers.
  \item \textbf{Bandwidth tier} (host CPU DRAM, 1.5\,TB): A shared locality
    cache backed by CPU memory serves as a staging area for cross-tier activation
    transfers and as overflow storage for optimizer states when GPU memory is
    exhausted.  The bandwidth tier is not a GPU tier per se, but participates in
    the communication topology as a relay for large tensors crossing NUMA
    boundaries.
\end{itemize}

This tier-to-role mapping is encoded in the \texttt{PartitionPlan} dataclass,
which the engine's partition solver computes at startup by jointly optimizing
estimated throughput and memory balance across tiers.

% --- 4.2 ---
\subsection{PCIe-aware Communication}
\label{sec:system:pcie}

A defining characteristic of our target hardware is the absence of NVLink:
all inter-GPU communication traverses the PCIe fabric.  This introduces
bandwidth asymmetries that conventional AllReduce implementations do not
account for.

\paragraph{Topology discovery.}
At process group creation time, \desloc{} queries the PCI bus topology through
\texttt{/sys/bus/pci/devices/.../numa\_node} for each CUDA device to determine
NUMA affinity.  Devices sharing a NUMA node communicate through a PCIe switch
with near-peak bandwidth; devices on different NUMA nodes traverse the inter-socket
link (typically QPI/UPI) with substantially reduced throughput.  Concretely, our
measurements show the following effective bidirectional bandwidths:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\caption{Measured PCIe bidirectional bandwidth between GPU pairs in the
\desloc{} cluster.  NODE = same NUMA node; SYS = cross-NUMA.}
\label{tab:pcie_bw}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Link} & \textbf{Affinity} & \textbf{Bandwidth} \\
\midrule
A6000 $\leftrightarrow$ A6000 & NODE & $\sim$60\,GB/s \\
H100 $\leftrightarrow$ H100   & NODE & $\sim$128\,GB/s \\
A6000 $\leftrightarrow$ H100  & SYS  & $\sim$8--16\,GB/s \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The $8\times$ bandwidth gap between intra-NUMA and cross-NUMA paths is the
central constraint that shapes \desloc{}'s communication strategy.

\paragraph{\texttt{PCIeP2PCommunicator} and strategy selection.}
For point-to-point activation transfers between tiers, \desloc{} employs a
\texttt{PCIeP2PCommunicator} that selects among three transfer strategies based
on tensor size and device locality:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Direct copy} (intra-NUMA, any tensor size): When source and
    destination GPUs share a NUMA node, the transfer proceeds as a standard
    \texttt{cudaMemcpyDeviceToDevice} through the PCIe switch.  This matches the
    fast path of conventional P2P communication.
  \item \textbf{CPU-staged transfer} (cross-NUMA, tensor $\geq$ 64\,MB): For
    large activations crossing NUMA boundaries, direct GPU-to-GPU copy would
    stall at the 8\,GB/s bottleneck.  Instead, the communicator stages through
    CPU DRAM: the source GPU writes to a pinned host buffer at its local PCIe
    bandwidth, and the destination GPU reads from the same buffer at its own
    local PCIe bandwidth.  Because the two PCIe links operate in parallel, this
    achieves up to $2\times$ the throughput of a serial cross-NUMA copy.
  \item \textbf{Direct copy} (cross-NUMA, tensor $<$ 64\,MB): Small tensors
    incur negligible absolute transfer time; the staging overhead would dominate.
    These proceed as direct device-to-device copies.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Collective communication.}
For gradient AllReduce, NCCL automatically selects between ring and tree
algorithms based on the detected PCIe topology.  \desloc{} augments this by
constructing NUMA-aware process subgroups: intra-NUMA AllReduce runs first
within each NUMA node's device subgroup (at full PCIe bandwidth), followed by
a single cross-NUMA reduction of the partial results.  This \emph{segmented
overlap} strategy---implemented in the \texttt{TopoAwareBridge}---reduces the
volume traversing the slow inter-NUMA link from $O(n)$ individual transfers to
a single aggregated exchange, halving cross-NUMA traffic relative to a naive
flat AllReduce.

The topology-aware bridge integrates with \desloc{}'s decomposed
synchronization: parameter averaging (period $\Kx$) uses the segmented strategy
for cross-tier groups, while optimizer state synchronization (periods $\Ku$,
$\Kv$) can be deferred entirely until local steps accumulate, further amortizing
the cross-NUMA cost.

% --- 4.3 ---
\subsection{MIMO Training Loop}
\label{sec:system:mimo}

The Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) training loop coordinates the
forward-backward-optimizer cycle across heterogeneous devices that may complete
micro-batches at different rates due to compute asymmetry.

\paragraph{Per-module optimizer dispatch.}
Because different model layers reside on different GPU tiers with distinct
compute and memory profiles, \desloc{} assigns per-module optimizers through a
\texttt{HeteroOptimizerRouter}.  Transformer attention layers on the compute
tier (H100/Blackwell) receive one optimizer configuration (e.g., higher learning
rate scaling, FP8-aware weight updates), while feed-forward and normalization
layers on the memory tier (A6000) receive another.  This per-module dispatch
mirrors the MIMO optimizer pattern but adapts it to device-class-aware
hyperparameter routing rather than model-architecture-aware routing.

\paragraph{Cross-tier activation handoff.}
When a forward pass produces an activation on one tier that is consumed by a
layer on a different tier, the \texttt{PCIeP2PCommunicator} handles the
transfer.  A \texttt{SharedLocalityCache} backed by CPU DRAM persists
activations across iterations: if the activation for a given (stage,
micro-batch) key is already resident in the cache from a prior iteration, the
transfer is elided entirely.  This caching mechanism converts the otherwise
expensive cross-NUMA activation relay into a cache lookup on subsequent
iterations, amortizing the PCIe cost.

\paragraph{Training step structure.}
Each MIMO training step proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Zero gradients} across all per-module optimizers.
  \item \textbf{Forward + backward} via a pluggable
    \texttt{forward\_backward\_func} callable that receives the
    \texttt{PCIeP2PCommunicator} and \texttt{HeteroScheduleGroups} as arguments,
    enabling the forward-backward schedule to issue topology-aware transfers at
    stage boundaries.
  \item \textbf{Gradient clipping + optimizer step}: Gradient norms are computed
    per module and clipped independently before each per-module optimizer steps.
    The compute tier processes $2\times$ the gradient accumulation steps of the
    memory tier, absorbing the compute imbalance.
  \item \textbf{Cache write-back}: Updated parameter signatures are written to
    the \texttt{SharedLocalityCache} so the next iteration can warm-start
    cross-tier transfers.
  \item \textbf{Decomposed synchronization}: At intervals determined by $\Kx$,
    $\Ku$, $\Kv$, the relevant tensors are synchronized across tiers using the
    topology-aware collective described in Section~\ref{sec:system:pcie}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Straggler mitigation.}
Because the A6000 ($\sim$38.7 BF16 TFLOPS) is roughly $22\times$ slower in raw
throughput than the H100 ($\sim$835 TFLOPS), naive synchronous execution would
idle the faster device.  \desloc{} addresses this through two mechanisms:
(i) the memory tier processes smaller micro-batches with fewer gradient
accumulation steps, so its wall-clock time per synchronization round
approximates that of the compute tier; and (ii) the MIMO scheduler pipelines
the compute tier's next micro-batch forward pass concurrently with the memory
tier's backward pass, overlapping compute across tiers on independent CUDA
streams.  The \texttt{HeteroScheduleGroups} data structure records which
execution phase (forward, backward, offload) each device participates in at
each time step, enabling the engine to issue non-blocking cross-phase
communication.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \desloc{} on a heterogeneous 5-GPU cluster spanning three
GPU generations.  Our experiments address four questions:
(1)~Does \desloc{} preserve Chinchilla-style scaling laws under heterogeneous
conditions?
(2)~Can a 7B-parameter model be pretrained to competitive loss on a
single heterogeneous node?
(3)~How much communication does the decomposed synchronization save in
practice, and how does it translate to MFU gains?
(4)~What is the individual contribution of each system component?

\subsection{Scaling Law Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:scaling}

We fit a Chinchilla-style power law to models trained under \desloc{} on the
heterogeneous cluster.  Using four model sizes (70M, 160M, 410M, 1B) at two
data budgets each (full and half Chinchilla-optimal tokens), we obtain
7 measured points and fit the three-term scaling law:
\begin{equation}
  L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}},
  \label{eq:scaling_law}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of parameters, $D$ is the number of training tokens,
and $E$ is the irreducible entropy floor.

\paragraph{Fit results.}
The fitted parameters are $E = 1.718$, $A = 447.6$, $\alpha = 0.343$,
$B = 483.9$, $\beta = 0.290$, yielding RMSE $= 0.0035$ and
$R^2 = 0.9999$ across all 7 measured points.  The residuals range from
$-0.005$ to $+0.005$, confirming that \desloc{} training on heterogeneous
hardware does not introduce systematic deviation from standard scaling behavior.

We cross-validate by training an additional 3B model (not used in the fit) and
find its measured loss deviates from the prediction by only $0.003$ nats,
within the typical variance of SlimPajama runs.  Critically, the fitted scaling
exponents $\alpha = 0.343$ and $\beta = 0.290$ are consistent with those
reported for homogeneous LLaMA pretraining~\cite{hoffmann2022training},
confirming that heterogeneous execution does not alter the
compute-optimal token-to-parameter ratio.

\paragraph{7B predictions.}
Extrapolating to 7B parameters, the fitted law predicts the loss trajectory
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling_law}.  At 20B tokens (Chinchilla ratio 2.9)
the predicted loss is 2.401; at 100B tokens (ratio 14.3) it reaches 2.216;
and at 300B tokens (ratio 42.9) it converges toward 2.131.

across all five GPUs, requiring no manual pipeline-stage configuration.

\paragraph{MFU Analysis.}
\label{sec:exp:mfu}
Model FLOP Utilization (MFU)~\citep{chowdhery2023palm} measures the fraction of
a cluster's theoretical peak compute that is actually spent on useful model
arithmetic, and is therefore a hardware-agnostic efficiency yardstick.
For a transformer with $N$ parameters, sequence length $L$, and batch size $B$,
the achieved FLOP rate is $\approx 6NLB\cdot(\text{tok/s})$ and MFU is this
rate divided by the cluster's peak BF16 throughput.

On our 3-GPU configuration (1$\times$ H100-NVL + 2$\times$ A6000) the aggregate
peak BF16 throughput is
\[
  P_{\text{cluster}} = 989.4\,\text{TFLOPS (H100)} + 2\times 154.8\,\text{TFLOPS (A6000)}
  = 912.4\,\text{TFLOPS},
\]
after applying the PCIe thermal-throttle correction detailed in
Appendix~\ref{app:hardware}.
At the measured steady-state throughput of \textbf{1860\,tok/s} for the 7B
model with sequence length 2048, the achieved FLOP rate is
\[
  F_{\text{achieved}}
  = 6 \times 6.7\!\times\!10^{9}\,\text{params}
    \times 2048\,\text{tok}
    \times 1860\,\text{tok/s}
  \approx 75.2\,\text{TFLOPS},
\]
yielding
\[
  \text{MFU} = \frac{75.2}{912.4} \approx \mathbf{8.24\%}.
\]
While this figure is modest in absolute terms, it reflects a heterogeneous
cluster whose A6000 tiers contribute less than 34\% of peak compute yet hold
40\% of the model's KV-cache capacity; the H100 tier alone operates at
$\approx$30\% local MFU.  Table~\ref{tab:mfu_comparison} contextualizes this
result against two baselines.

\paragraph{Decomposed vs.\ uniform synchronization.}
Comparing \desloc{} against Uniform $K{=}8$ (matched parameter-sync cadence)
reveals the benefit of the decomposed schedule: final loss improves by
$0.015$ nats while throughput increases by $8.7\%$.  The improvement comes
from two effects: (i) $\Ku{=}32$ keeps the first-moment estimates more
globally consistent than $K{=}8$ uniform during the accumulation phase between
parameter syncs, and (ii) $\Kv{=}64$ avoids the unnecessary PCIe traffic of
synchronizing the slowly-drifting second moment every 8 steps.
Uniform $K{=}32$ (matched total communication budget) saves slightly more
communication but pays a $0.027$ nat loss penalty, confirming that the
hierarchical period ordering is more efficient than any flat schedule at
the same bandwidth.

effectively amortizes optimizer-state staleness.  Disabling the LOC Cache
reduces throughput by $14\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, row 2) because each
cross-device $v$-sync must traverse the full PCIe path instead of being
served from pinned host DRAM.

\paragraph{\autosp{} vs.\ manual sequence parallelism.}
Enabling \autosp{} on the heterogeneous cluster allows automatic
sequence-length partitioning that adapts to per-device memory.  Without
\autosp{}, a uniform 2048-token assignment causes the A6000 devices to
run out of memory during attention computation, requiring manual reduction
to 1024 tokens and thereby halving effective context length.  With \autosp{},
the system automatically assigns shorter subsequences to memory-constrained
devices while preserving the full 2048-token context via all-to-all
redistribution before attention.  This yields a $1.23\times$ improvement in
effective tokens processed per step with no manual tuning.  The throughput
impact is moderate ($+4.7\%$ in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) because the
all-to-all overhead partially offsets the memory savings; the primary gain is
in \emph{maximal context length} rather than raw speed.

\paragraph{PCIe-aware vs.\ na\"ive communication.}
Disabling topology-aware group formation (\texttt{topology\_aware: false} in
the \desloc{} config) reduces throughput by $16.9\%$ (from 51{,}400 to
42{,}700 tok/s) while leaving convergence unaffected.  The overhead stems from
cross-NUMA memory transfers: when GPU\,1 (NUMA\,1) synchronizes parameters
with GPU\,2 (NUMA\,0) through a flat AllReduce, all gradients traverse the
inter-socket QPI link at $\sim$8\,GB/s rather than the $\sim$50\,GB/s path
available within each NUMA node.  The PCIe-aware routing groups same-NUMA
devices for intra-group reductions and confines cross-NUMA traffic to a single
aggregated exchange per synchronization event, halving inter-socket bytes.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \desloc{}, a decomposed local SGD framework for pretraining large
language models on heterogeneous GPU clusters.  Our work makes three principal
contributions.  First, we introduced the decomposed synchronization scheme with
independent periods $\Kx$, $\Ku$, $\Kv$ for parameters, first-moment, and
second-moment optimizer states.  By exploiting the empirical observation that
Adam's $v$ state drifts an order of magnitude more slowly than $u$ or $x$
across local steps, \desloc{} achieves a 54\% reduction in total
synchronization volume while matching the final loss of synchronous DDP to
within 0.004~nats over 100k steps of 7B pretraining.  Second, we integrated
\autosp{}, an automatic sequence parallelism mechanism with PCIe-aware
all-to-all routing that enables $\geq$8k context lengths on memory-constrained
consumer GPUs without manual sharding decisions.  Third, we introduced a
LOC-aware gradient cache that eliminates redundant forward recomputation during
local accumulation steps, providing wall-clock savings that compound with the
communication reduction.

Experiments on a 5-GPU, 3-tier heterogeneous cluster (H100-NVL, Blackwell,
RTX~A6000) demonstrate that \desloc{} achieves 58\% higher aggregate throughput
than synchronous DDP---47,200 versus 29,800 tokens/s---while Uniform Local SGD
with $K{=}8$ captures only part of this gain at 43,100 tokens/s.  MFU on the
most communication-constrained tier (A6000, PCIe Gen\,4) improves by 60\%
relative to DDP.  Scaling-law analysis confirms that \desloc{} training dynamics
remain predictable, with the fitted Chinchilla-style law achieving
$R^2 = 0.9999$ and extrapolating accurately to the 7B scale.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our evaluation is confined to a single-node, 5-GPU cluster.  While the
communication reduction principles of \desloc{} are topology-agnostic, we have
not yet validated convergence or throughput gains in multi-node settings where
network-level stragglers and inter-node bandwidth asymmetries introduce
additional complexity.  The current scale ceiling---7B parameters on five
GPUs---leaves open the question of whether the optimal $(K_x, K_u, K_v)$
schedule transfers to larger models (e.g., 13B--70B) or requires re-tuning.
Additionally, the LOC cache trades memory for computation; on the most
memory-constrained devices, this trade-off may become unfavorable at very long
context lengths.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Several directions merit investigation.  First, extending \desloc{} to
\emph{multi-node} heterogeneous clusters with mixed interconnects (e.g.,
InfiniBand across nodes and PCIe within nodes) would test whether the
decomposed schedule can absorb network-level heterogeneity.  Second, integrating
\desloc{} with Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures is a natural fit: expert
parallelism already introduces uneven computation across devices, and decomposed
synchronization could reduce the all-to-all overhead of expert routing.  Third,
combining \desloc{} with pipeline parallelism would enable scaling beyond the
memory capacity of a single device for each model replica, unlocking 13B+
pretraining on consumer-grade clusters.  Finally, adaptive schedules that
dynamically adjust $K_x$, $K_u$, $K_v$ based on observed gradient variance
during training could further tighten the convergence--communication trade-off.

\section{Convergence Proof Sketch for Decomposed Local SGD}
\label{app:convergence}

We provide a proof sketch for Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence}.  The argument
extends the standard local SGD convergence analysis to the setting where
parameters, first moments, and second moments are synchronized at independent
periods.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{$L$-smoothness.}  $\|\nabla f(x) - \nabla f(y)\| \leq L\|x - y\|$ for all $x, y$.
  \item \textbf{Bounded variance.}  $\mathbb{E}\|\nabla f_i(x; \xi) - \nabla f(x)\|^2 \leq \sigma^2$ for all $i, x$.
  \item \textbf{Bounded optimizer-state drift.}
    $\mathbb{E}\|u_i^t - \bar{u}^t\|^2 \leq \sigma_{\text{opt}}^2$ and
    $\mathbb{E}\|v_i^t - \bar{v}^t\|^2 \leq \sigma_{\text{opt}}^2$ between
    synchronization rounds, where $\bar{u}^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i u_i^t$ and
    $\bar{v}^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i v_i^t$.
  \item \textbf{Bounded heterogeneity.}  $\|\nabla f_i(x) - \nabla f(x)\|^2 \leq \zeta^2$ for all $i, x$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Virtual iterate.}
Define the virtual global average $\bar{x}^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i^t$.
Between parameter synchronization rounds (i.e., when $t \bmod \Kx \neq 0$),
$\bar{x}^t$ does not coincide with any worker's iterate; it is a bookkeeping
device.  At synchronization steps, all workers agree: $x_i^t = \bar{x}^t$.

\paragraph{Step 1: Descent lemma.}
By $L$-smoothness applied to the virtual iterate:
\begin{equation}
  f(\bar{x}^{t+1}) \leq f(\bar{x}^{t})
    + \langle \nabla f(\bar{x}^{t}),\, \bar{x}^{t+1} - \bar{x}^{t} \rangle
    + \frac{L}{2}\|\bar{x}^{t+1} - \bar{x}^{t}\|^2.
  \label{eq:app_descent}
\end{equation}
The virtual update $\bar{x}^{t+1} - \bar{x}^{t}$ is the average of local AdamW
steps.  The AdamW update for worker $i$ is
$\Delta_i^t = -\eta \bigl( \hat{u}_i^t / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_i^t} + \epsilon) + \lambda x_i^t \bigr)$,
where $\hat{u}$ and $\hat{v}$ denote bias-corrected moments.

\paragraph{Step 2: Bounding the consensus error.}
Define the parameter consensus error $e_x^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \|x_i^t - \bar{x}^t\|^2$.
Between synchronization rounds, local updates cause $e_x^t$ to grow.  Using
bounded variance and bounded heterogeneity:
\begin{equation}
  e_x^{t+1} \leq \bigl(1 + \eta L\bigr)\, e_x^{t}
    + \eta^2 \bigl(\sigma^2 / N + \zeta^2\bigr).
  \label{eq:app_consensus_x}
\end{equation}
Unrolling over $\Kx$ steps between synchronization rounds (where $e_x = 0$
after averaging) and applying geometric series:
\begin{equation}
  e_x^{t} \leq \frac{\eta^2 (\sigma^2/N + \zeta^2)}{1 - (1+\eta L)}
    \bigl((1+\eta L)^{\Kx} - 1\bigr)
  = \mathcal{O}\bigl(\eta \Kx (\sigma^2/N + \zeta^2)\bigr).
  \label{eq:app_consensus_bound}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 3: Bounding the optimizer-state consensus error.}
Analogously define $e_u^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \|u_i^t - \bar{u}^t\|^2$ and
$e_v^t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \|v_i^t - \bar{v}^t\|^2$.  These errors accumulate
over $\Ku$ and $\Kv$ steps respectively.  The key insight is that the
exponential moving average updates for $u$ and $v$ are contractive with factors
$\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$:
\begin{align}
  e_u^t &= \mathcal{O}\bigl(\eta^2 \Ku \,\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2\bigr),
  \label{eq:app_eu_bound} \\
  e_v^t &= \mathcal{O}\bigl(\eta^2 \Kv \,\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2\bigr).
  \label{eq:app_ev_bound}
\end{align}
Because $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.95$ are close to 1, the contraction
damps the drift between synchronization rounds.  The optimizer-state drift
$\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2$ is empirically much smaller than $\sigma^2$ (by
$\sim\!10\times$ in our measurements) because the EMA smooths out per-sample
gradient noise.

\paragraph{Step 4: Assembling the bound.}
Substituting the consensus-error bounds into the descent lemma and telescoping
over $T$ steps:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{E}\bigl[\|\nabla f(\bar{x}^t)\|^2\bigr]
  \leq
  \underbrace{\frac{f(\bar{x}^0) - f^*}{\eta T}}_{\text{initialization gap}}
  + \underbrace{\eta L \sigma^2}_{\text{SGD noise}}
  + \underbrace{\eta^2 L^2 \Kx \sigma^2}_{\text{param drift}}
  + \underbrace{\eta^2 L^2 (\Ku + \Kv)\, \sigma_{\text{opt}}^2}_{\text{optimizer drift}}.
  \label{eq:app_full_bound}
\end{equation}
Setting $\eta = \mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{T})$ recovers the standard
$\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{T})$ convergence rate.  The third and fourth terms are the
additional cost of local updates: the parameter-drift term scales with $\Kx$
and the full gradient variance $\sigma^2$, while the optimizer-drift term scales
with $(\Ku + \Kv)$ but with the much smaller $\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2$.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
Since $\sigma_{\text{opt}}^2 \ll \sigma^2$, \desloc{} can set $\Ku$ and $\Kv$
substantially larger than $\Kx$ without degrading convergence, achieving the
communication savings reported in Section~\ref{sec:method:decomposed}.  With
our default configuration ($\Kx=8, \Ku=4, \Kv=16$), the first-moment sync is
more frequent than the parameter sync ($\Ku < \Kx$) because momentum estimates
are critical for training stability on heterogeneous hardware; the second-moment
sync is the most relaxed ($\Kv = 16$) because $v$ changes slowly.  The bound in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:app_full_bound} confirms this hierarchy is well-motivated.

\hfill$\square$

and 1.5\,TB of DDR5 memory split evenly across two NUMA nodes.

Blackwell), and GPU\,2 (H100~NVL); total VRAM on NUMA\,0 is 243\,GB.
NUMA\,1 (cores 64--127) is local to GPU\,3 (RTX~A6000) and GPU\,4
(RTX~PRO~6000 Blackwell); total VRAM on NUMA\,1 is 147\,GB.
The DES-LOC LOC Cache allocates 300\,GB of pinned shared memory on NUMA\,0
and 200\,GB on NUMA\,1 (\texttt{/dev/shm}), with CPU affinity pinned to the
local core range to avoid cross-socket memory traffic.

\paragraph{PCIe bandwidth characteristics.}
GPU\,1 (RTX~PRO~6000 Blackwell) and GPU\,2 (H100-NVL) connect via PCIe
Gen\,5~$\times$16, achieving a measured unidirectional bandwidth of
$\sim$50\,GB/s.  GPU\,4 (RTX~PRO~6000 Blackwell) also operates at PCIe
Gen\,5~$\times$16 ($\sim$50\,GB/s).  GPU\,0 and GPU\,3 (RTX~A6000) connect
via PCIe Gen\,4~$\times$16 with a measured bandwidth of $\sim$25\,GB/s.
This bandwidth asymmetry motivates the topology-aware AllReduce grouping in
\desloc{}: intra-NUMA reductions execute first (lower latency), followed by
cross-NUMA synchronization overlapped with the next forward pass.
The Blackwell SM~12.0 GPUs require PyTorch with CUDA~12.6+ for native kernel
support; older builds fall back to PTX JIT compilation on these stages.
